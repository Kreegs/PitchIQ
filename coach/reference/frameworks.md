# Sales Frameworks

These are the specific techniques Veriforge reps are trained on. Rex references these during briefings (to orient reps before a call) and debriefs (to evaluate what happened). This is not a textbook — it is a practical reference for the four methods the coach applies.

---

## Outcome-First Opening Structure

Every call and email opener must state what changes for the prospect before stating what the product does.

**Structure:**
1. Prospect's name — once, in the first sentence
2. A specific outcome or pain point relevant to their role and industry
3. One short diagnostic question to confirm relevance before going further

**Pass example:**
"Maria, manufacturers running three or more production lines are losing an average of 12 to 15 percent of capacity to unplanned downtime — and most can't see where it's coming from until the line stops. Is that something you're managing at Mercer?"

**Fail example:**
"Maria, I'm calling from Veriforge — we're a real-time manufacturing intelligence platform that uses IoT sensors and machine learning to optimise your production line and reduce inefficiencies across your operation..."

The fail example describes the product. The pass example describes the prospect's problem. Rex evaluates openers on this distinction alone. Features are not outcomes. "We use AI" is not a reason a plant manager should care.

## The Acknowledge-Reframe Method

Every objection must be acknowledged before it is answered. Barreling through an objection — restating the pitch immediately after a prospect pushes back — tells the prospect they are not being heard. It is the single fastest way to lose a call that could have been saved.

**Structure:**
1. Acknowledge the objection in plain terms. Do not parrot it back. Do not say "great question" or "I totally understand."
2. Ask a diagnostic question if the objection is vague or surface-level, to understand what is actually behind it.
3. Reframe toward the prospect's real concern — not the surface objection.

**Example — "We already have a system for that":**
Acknowledge: "Got it — what are you running?"
Diagnose: "What does it cover well, and where does it fall short for you?"
Reframe: "That's typically where we come in — not to replace what's working, but to give you visibility into what isn't."

**What it is not:**
"I hear you, but our platform is actually different because..." is not the Acknowledge-Reframe method. "But" cancels everything before it. The prospect hears the dismissal, not the acknowledgment. Rex evaluates whether the acknowledgment was genuine before the reframe — not just whether the word "acknowledge" appeared.

## The Three Strikes Rule

If the same objection appears three times in a single call, the rep must change approach on the third instance. Repetition is not persuasion. Repeating the same response louder or with different words is a Tier 1 failure.

**First instance:** Handle with the Acknowledge-Reframe method.

**Second instance:** Note that the objection has come back. Go deeper before responding. "It sounds like [objection] is the real sticking point here — can you help me understand what's behind it? Is it timing, or something else?"

**Third instance:** The rep must shift strategy entirely. Three options:

Concede and plant a seed: "If that's not going to move right now, I understand. Is there a point in the future where that changes — and would it be okay to stay in touch until then?"

Pivot to a different angle: Change the subject entirely and look for a different opening. "Setting that aside for a second — what would need to be true for a conversation like this to be worth your time?"

Exit cleanly: Take the loss gracefully and leave the door open. "That's fair. I don't want to waste your time. Would it be okay if I reached out again in Q3 when things open up?"

What the rep cannot do on the third strike: repeat the same response again. The rule exists because prospects who hear the same answer three times have stopped listening. A different approach — even a worse one — is better than repetition.

## Defined Next Step Formula

Every call and email must end with a next step that meets all three criteria:

**[Specific action] + [Specific date] + [Who is responsible]**

**Pass examples:**
"I'll send the IT security brief to your inbox by Thursday. Can we put 20 minutes on the calendar for Monday the 14th — you, me, and whoever from your IT team makes sense?"

"Let me book a demo slot for your operations team. Does Tuesday the 9th at 2pm work, or is later in the week better for you?"

**Fail examples:**
"I'll follow up with some information." — No date. No specific action. No commitment from the prospect.
"Let's stay in touch." — Not a next step.
"I'll send something over." — What? When? Who follows up if there is no response?

The next step must be agreed to by the prospect, not just stated by the rep. If the prospect says "sure, send it over" and the rep does not lock down a date or a response expectation, it is a soft close at best and a Tier 1 failure at worst.

For cold email: the next step is a response the prospect commits to giving. "If you're open to a call, just reply with a time" is a soft ask. "I'll reach out Thursday to see if a 20-minute call makes sense" locks in a specific follow-up action with a date and a responsible party — the rep.
