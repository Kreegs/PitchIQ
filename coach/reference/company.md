## Company Name

Veriforge

## What We Sell
<!-- REQUIRED: Describe the product in plain language. Avoid jargon.
     Focus on what the customer gets, not how it works technically. -->
A SaaS platform that connects to existing manufacturing equipment and gives plant managers real-time visibility into line efficiency, downtime causes, and maintenance predictions. Veriforge does not replace existing equipment. It makes the machinery already on the floor smarter and more transparent.

## Who We Sell To (ICP)
<!-- REQUIRED: Be specific. Include job titles, company size, and the primary pain point.
     This tells Rex who the rep is calling and what they care about. -->
Operations directors and plant managers at mid-size manufacturers (200 to 2000 employees). Companies running at least two production lines. Primary pain: unplanned downtime and reactive maintenance costing 15 to 20 percent of production capacity. Secondary pain: no line-level visibility into where the losses are coming from until it is too late.

## Core Value Proposition
<!-- REQUIRED: The headline outcome and proof point.
     This is what Rex anchors on in briefings and what reps should lead with. -->
Manufacturers using Veriforge reduce unplanned downtime by an average of 34 percent in the first 90 days. The platform pays for itself in the first quarter or the customer gets their money back.

## Common Objections and Approved Responses
<!-- REQUIRED: List the objections reps will most commonly face and how to approach them.
     Rex uses these during briefings to prep reps and during debriefs to evaluate responses.
     Describe the approach, not a word-for-word script — reps need to think, not recite. -->

**"We already have a system for that."**
Ask what system, then ask what it does not cover. Never dismiss what they have. The goal is to find the gap, not to position against what is working. "What system are you running? And where does it fall short for you?"

**"We don't have budget right now."**
Anchor to the cost of downtime before talking about Veriforge's cost. "What does an hour of unplanned downtime cost your operation?" Make the pain concrete. Price becomes easier to discuss when the cost of the problem is on the table.

**"We'd need to get IT involved."**
Validate it. "That's the right call — we have an IT-ready security brief I can send directly to your team. When's a good time to get them on a 20-minute call with us?" Turn the objection into a logical next step rather than a blocker.

**"We're not ready to make a change right now."**
Ask about their timeline. Get a specific date. Plant a seed for a future follow-up with a concrete touchpoint — not a vague "I'll check in." "When does that window open up? Q3? End of year?"

**"Send me some information."**
Clarify what specifically they want to know before committing to send anything. "What would make the information worth your time to read — is it the technical integration side, or more the ROI numbers?" Avoid sending a generic deck.

## Brand Standards
<!-- REQUIRED: Non-negotiable rules for how reps represent the company.
     Rex enforces these during debrief evaluations. -->

- Refer to the company as Veriforge. Do not use "we" without context in written communications.
- Never promise implementation timelines that cannot be guaranteed.
- Never compare directly to a named competitor. Acknowledge competitors exist and redirect to Veriforge's differentiation.
- Tone: professional but conversational. Confident, never desperate. Not stiff or scripted.
- Price is never discussed on a first call. If pressed: "That depends on your setup — that's exactly what the scoping call would nail down."
