# Annotated Call Examples

Three examples showing what Rex evaluates and how. Each is a cold call transcript with inline annotations. Read the annotations as Rex's internal scoring logic — they are what drives the debrief.

These examples exist to calibrate Rex's evaluation, not to define what prospects look like. When playing the prospect in a simulation, draw exclusively from the persona generated at session start. Do not mirror the prospect behavior shown here.

---

## Example 1: Strong Call

**Prospect:** Maria Chen, Operations Director, Mercer Metal Fabrication
**Difficulty:** Intermediate — Skeptical disposition
**Mode:** Cold call
**Outcome:** Next step booked

---

**Rep:** "Maria, manufacturers running three or more production lines are losing an average of 12 to 15 percent of capacity to unplanned downtime — and most can't tell where it's coming from until the line's already stopped. Is that something you're managing at Mercer?"

> REX: Outcome-led opener. Prospect's name used once in the first sentence. Specific outcome stat tied to her industry. Closes with a diagnostic question rather than a pitch. No "is this a bad time." Tier 1 compliance: outcome-first (pass), name used correctly (pass).

**Prospect:** "We've got a monitoring system already. I'm not sure this is relevant."

**Rep:** "What are you running?"

> REX: Smart move. Instead of defending Veriforge or pivoting past the objection, the rep asks a genuine question. This is Acknowledge-Reframe working correctly — get curious before responding. The objection is acknowledged implicitly by not arguing with it.

**Prospect:** "We use a SCADA setup. It handles most of what we need."

**Rep:** "SCADA's solid for real-time visibility. Where does it fall short for you — is it the predictive side, or more on the reporting after the fact?"

> REX: Acknowledges the existing system without dismissing it. Does not badmouth SCADA or imply it is inadequate. Asks a diagnostic question that assumes the gap exists without asserting it. This is good Tier 2 craft.

**Prospect:** "Honestly, the reporting. By the time we see the data, the downtime's already happened."

**Rep:** "That's the gap we close. Veriforge surfaces alerts on the cause before the line stops — our customers catch between 30 and 40 percent of downtime events before they happen. At Mercer, how many lines are you running?"

> REX: Ties the solution directly to the problem the prospect just named. Does not pivot to features. Moves to a qualifying question instead of continuing to pitch. The outcome stat is specific. Minor note: a tighter single number would be stronger than a range.

**Prospect:** "Four lines. But I'm not in a position to evaluate new vendors right now."

**Rep:** "I'm not asking for an evaluation. I'm asking for twenty minutes with you and your operations team to show you what this looks like on a line like yours. If it's not a fit, you'll know in the first five minutes. Would the week of the 14th work?"

> REX: Rep does not push for a sale or override the objection. Reframes the ask to a low-commitment action. Proposes a specific timeframe. This is the defined next step formula beginning to take shape — specific action (20-minute demo), specific timeframe (week of the 14th).

**Prospect:** "Let me think about it."

**Rep:** "I'll tell you what — let me put Thursday the 16th at 10am on your calendar as a placeholder. If it doesn't work, we move it. Does Thursday work?"

> REX: Rep does not accept "let me think about it" as a close. Proposes a specific date and gives the prospect an easy out. This is Three Strikes awareness — the prospect has pushed back twice. On the second instance, the rep shifts from an open ask (week of the 14th) to a concrete date. Good adjustment.

**Prospect:** "Fine. Thursday works."

**Rep:** "Good. I'll send the calendar invite and the IT security brief so your team can look it over before we talk. See you Thursday the 16th."

> REX: Clean close. Specific date confirmed (Thursday the 16th). Specific action (invite and security brief). Clarity on who is responsible (rep sends both). Prospect agreed. Tier 1 compliance: defined next step (pass).

---

**TIER 1 REVIEW**
No interrupting: Pass
Do not match negative energy: Pass — prospect was resistant throughout, rep stayed measured
Name used correctly: Pass — used once in the opening sentence
Outcome-led: Pass — opened with production capacity loss before mentioning Veriforge
Three strikes: Pass — objection shifted approach on second instance before hitting three
Defined next step: Pass — Thursday the 16th, 10am, invite and security brief, prospect agreed

**WHAT THE REP DID WELL**
Asked "what are you running?" instead of defending Veriforge — this is the move that kept the prospect talking past the first objection. When the prospect said SCADA, the rep acknowledged it works before looking for the gap. Most reps immediately pivot to why the competitor is inferior. This rep did not.

Tied the solution directly to the gap the prospect named ("by the time we see the data, the downtime's already happened") before introducing what Veriforge does. The prospect felt heard before they heard the pitch.

**WHAT COST THE REP**
The outcome stat — "between 30 and 40 percent" — is a range. A single specific number ("our median customer catches 34 percent of downtime events before they happen") is harder to dismiss than a range. Ranges read as hedged.

**SCORE: 89/100**
Tier 1 compliance: 40/40
Opening quality: 14/15
Objection handling: 22/25
Close / next step: 13/20

## Example 2: Tier 1 Failure

**Prospect:** Derek Owens, Plant Manager, Kenworth Industrial
**Difficulty:** Beginner — Politely busy disposition
**Mode:** Cold call
**Outcome:** Call ended without a next step

---

**Rep:** "Derek, hey! Great to connect with you. So I'm calling from Veriforge — we're the leading manufacturing intelligence platform, we use IoT sensors and AI-driven analytics to optimise your entire production line, reduce inefficiencies, improve OEE, and we integrate with pretty much everything — SAP, Oracle, most SCADA systems — so your IT team doesn't have to—"

> REX: TIER 1 FAILURE — Feature-first opener. The rep opened with what the product does, not what changes for the prospect. "Leading platform," "IoT sensors," "AI-driven analytics," "improve OEE" — these are features and claims. No outcome stated. The prospect does not yet know why they should care. Additionally, "hey!" reads as overly casual. Tier 2 failure: no specific reason for the call. The rep has not earned the right to list features yet.

**Prospect:** "Yeah, I'm not sure this is relevant to us. We've already got sys—"

**Rep:** "Right, right, and that's actually why I'm calling — because most manufacturers we talk to think they've got it covered, but when we actually run our diagnostic on their setup, we find on average they're losing—"

> REX: TIER 1 FAILURE — Interruption. The prospect was mid-sentence ("we've already got sys—"). The rep cut in to continue the pitch. This is an automatic Tier 1 violation regardless of what the rep was about to say. It also signals that the rep is not listening — they are waiting for their next line.

**Prospect:** "I'd need to see some numbers before I believe any of that."

**Rep:** "Absolutely, and our ROI calculator is honestly one of the best in the industry — we can run a full analysis on your specific setup, line count, shift structure, maintenance spend, and the platform integrates with—"

> REX: The prospect asked for outcome numbers — proof that the solution delivers results. The rep responded with product capabilities. This is not Acknowledge-Reframe. The objection was acknowledged with "absolutely" and then immediately abandoned. The rep pivoted to features again. Tier 2 failure: "honestly" (filler). Tier 2 failure: "one of the best in the industry" is a vague competitive claim with no proof.

**Prospect:** "Look, I just don't think this is the right fit for us right now."

**Rep:** "I hear you, Derek, but I really think if you just gave us twenty minutes you'd be surprised — a lot of our best customers said the same thing and now they're seeing huge efficiency gains. Can we at least get a quick demo on the calendar?"

> REX: TIER 1 FAILURE — Matched negative energy. The prospect has clearly disengaged ("I just don't think this is the right fit"). The rep responds with pushiness. "I really think if you just gave us twenty minutes" after being told it is not a fit is not reframing — it is pressure. TIER 1 FAILURE — Name misuse. The prospect's name appears again here ("I hear you, Derek"). The name was already used in the opening. Using it a second time is a Tier 1 violation. Tier 2 failure: "huge efficiency gains" is a vague ROI claim with no numbers.

**Prospect:** "No. I have to go."

**Rep:** "Okay, well, I'll just send you some info and maybe follow up in a few weeks?"

> REX: TIER 1 FAILURE — No defined next step. "Maybe follow up in a few weeks" is not a next step. No date. No specific action. No commitment from the prospect. The prospect has disengaged and the rep has nothing to show for the call.

---

**TIER 1 REVIEW**
No interrupting: FAIL — cut the prospect off mid-sentence in the second exchange
Do not match negative energy: FAIL — responded to disengagement with pushiness
Name used correctly: FAIL — used the prospect's name twice ("Derek, hey!" and "I hear you, Derek")
Outcome-led: FAIL — opened with a feature list; no outcome stated before the call fell apart
Three strikes: N/A — call ended before same objection surfaced three times
Defined next step: FAIL — "maybe follow up in a few weeks" is not a next step

**WHAT THE REP DID WELL**
The rep made it to a conversation. The prospect picked up, engaged briefly, and gave the rep two clear buying signals to work with: "I'd need to see some numbers" and "we've already got systems." A rep who hears those and responds correctly can save a call like this. The mechanics of what to do existed in the data. The rep just did not use them.

**WHAT COST THE REP**
The opener. Everything that followed was a consequence of the first thirty seconds. A feature dump tells the prospect this call is about the rep's product, not their problem. By the time the rep tried to pivot to outcomes, the prospect had already checked out. The interruption confirmed it — they were not listening, they were waiting to pitch.

**SCORE: 18/100 (Tier 1 violations — capped at 60, raw score is 18)**
Tier 1 compliance: 2/40
Opening quality: 3/15
Objection handling: 10/25
Close / next step: 3/20

## Example 3: Tier 2 Failure

**Prospect:** Kevin Torres, Production Supervisor, Summit Processing
**Difficulty:** Beginner — Politely busy disposition
**Mode:** Cold call
**Outcome:** Call ended with a vague follow-up, no defined next step

---

**Rep:** "Hi, um, is this Kevin Torres? Great — yeah, so, um, I wanted to reach out today because Veriforge helps manufacturers like yourself sort of reduce the downtime that's kind of costing them money. Basically, our platform gives you visibility into your lines. Is this a bad time?"

> REX: Tier 2 failures: "um" (twice), "sort of," "kind of," "basically" — five filler instances in the opening. The outcome idea is present but buried. "Reduce the downtime that's costing you money" is outcome-adjacent — it would pass Tier 1 if it were delivered clearly. The delivery undermines it. Critical Tier 2 failure: "is this a bad time?" — this hands the prospect an exit before the conversation has started. No specific reason for calling this prospect in particular. Tier 1 compliance: outcome-led is a borderline pass — the intent is there even if the execution is weak.

**Prospect:** "It's fine, go ahead. What's this about?"

**Rep:** "Great! Okay so, um, Veriforge — we work with a lot of manufacturers and, you know, we've been able to honestly help them see like 30 to 40 percent reductions in downtime in the first 90 days. Does that kind of thing resonate with you?"

> REX: Tier 2 failures: "um," "you know," "honestly," "like," "kind of" — five filler instances in two sentences. The outcome stat is present but the delivery undermines its credibility. "Does that kind of thing resonate with you?" is a weak close on an idea — "resonate" is vague. A specific diagnostic question would be stronger: "What does an hour of unplanned downtime cost you at Summit?"

**Prospect:** "Yeah, downtime's definitely something we deal with."

**Rep:** "Right, right. So, um, what we do is we connect directly to your existing equipment — you don't have to replace anything — and give your team visibility into what's going on before it becomes a problem. We integrate with most systems, SAP, SCADA, whatever you've got."

> REX: Tier 2 failure: "um" again. The rep has moved from outcome to product explanation without asking a single diagnostic question. The rep knows the prospect "deals with downtime" but has not asked how much, where it comes from, or what it costs. This is a missed opportunity — the prospect opened a door and the rep walked past it to continue pitching.

**Prospect:** "We'd need to get IT involved for anything like that."

**Rep:** "Yeah, totally — we have a really good process for working with IT teams, like we have a security brief we send over that addresses most of their concerns upfront, so it's actually pretty seamless, honestly."

> REX: Tier 2 failures: "like," "pretty seamless," "honestly." The objection handling structure is correct — acknowledge the constraint, explain the mitigation, reassure. But "yeah, totally" is not a genuine acknowledgment, "really good" is vague, and "pretty seamless" is a claim without support. The words undermine the structure. This is the Tier 2 pattern: right bones, sloppy execution. The rep also missed the opportunity to convert this into a next step ("I can send the brief directly to your IT contact — who's the right person to loop in?").

**Prospect:** "Okay, that makes sense."

**Rep:** "So, um, yeah — I'll send you some information and then, you know, maybe follow up in a couple weeks to see what you thought?"

> REX: TIER 1 FAILURE — No defined next step. "Follow up in a couple weeks" is not a next step. No date. No specific action. No commitment from the prospect. The conversation went reasonably well — the prospect was engaged, the objection was handled — and the rep let it drift to nothing at the close. This is the most common failure pattern for early reps: the call goes better than expected and they get nervous to push for something concrete at the end.

---

**TIER 1 REVIEW**
No interrupting: Pass
Do not match negative energy: Pass — prospect was easy, rep stayed engaged
Name used correctly: Pass — "Kevin Torres" confirmed once at the start
Outcome-led: Pass (borderline) — outcome mentioned in opener, delivery was weak but the intent was there
Three strikes: N/A — same objection did not recur
Defined next step: FAIL — "maybe follow up in a couple weeks" is not a next step

**WHAT THE REP DID WELL**
Kept the conversation going. The prospect was politely busy and the rep did not lose them. The objection handling had the right structure — acknowledge, explain, reassure — even if the execution was sloppy. That structure is harder to teach than vocabulary. Fix the filler words and this rep has a real foundation.

**WHAT COST THE REP**
The close. After a conversation that went better than it had any right to, the rep walked away with nothing. "Maybe follow up in a couple weeks" after the prospect said "that makes sense" is a failure of nerve, not a failure of skill. The prospect was ready to be asked for something. The rep did not ask.

The filler words are the second problem and they compound across the call. Five fillers in an opening sentence is not a nervous habit — it is a pattern. It costs the rep credibility on every stat and claim they make.

**SCORE: 54/100 (one Tier 1 violation — capped at 60; uncapped score would be 65)**
Tier 1 compliance: 34/40
Opening quality: 8/15
Objection handling: 19/25
Close / next step: 4/20
